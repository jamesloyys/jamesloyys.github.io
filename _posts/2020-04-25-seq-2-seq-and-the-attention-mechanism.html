Seq2Seq Models in NLP

Neural networks and deep learning have proven to be powerful algorithms for solving a wide range of problems in AI, particularly in Computer Vision based problems such as image recognition. However, one drawback of conventional neural networks is that they require a fix length input. This is a stumbling block in NLP, as tasks such as machine translation and speech recognition have variable length inputs that are not known a-priori.

To that end, Seq2Seq models were introduced by Sutskever et al to tackle this problem. Seq2Seq models are neural networks that takes in a variable length sequence as input, and outputs another variable length sequence.

At its core, Seq2Seq models consist of an Encoder and a Decoder component. The role of the Encoder is to learn a compressed representation of the input sequences. This compressed representation is also known as the context vector. The decoder then picks up this context vector, and outputs a sequence.

To solidify our understand, let's take a closer look at how Seq2Seq models uses the encoder and decoder to translate a sentence from english to spanish.

Looking at the diagram below, the input to the model is the english sentence 'How are you'. The encoder processes the sentence word by word, until it reaches the end of the sentence. The <EOS> tag is a special tag that informs the encoder that the end of the sentence has been reached. Once the encoder sees the <EOS> tag, the encoder outputs the context vector.

The context vector contains the compressed representation of the input sentence. The decoder now reads the context vector, and begins to produce the output sequence word by word. Again, the <EOS> tag is used to denote the end of the output sequence. The decoder ends when it outputs the <EOS> tag. Note that the length of the input sentence can be different from the length of the output sentence.

Note that \(h_1\) refers to the hidden state of the encoder at time step \(i\), \(c\) refers to the context vector, and \(s_1\) refers to the hidden state of the decoder at time step \(i\).

In this model, the output at each time step \(y_t\) is conditional on the context vector \(c\) and the output from previous time steps. At each time step, we calculate the probability \( p(y_t|c,y_1,y_2,y_{t-1}) \) for each word in the vocabulary, and we use a softmax function to greedily select the word with the greatest score at each time step.

The Information Bottleneck in Seq2Seq Models

One significant disadvantage of the vanilla Seq2Seq model is that it depends entirely on the context vector to capture the symantics of the input sentence. This poses a problem for long sentences, as long term dependencies at the start of the sentence tends to be lost during the encoding process. This problem is known as the information bottleneck in Seq2Seq models.

Paying attention to the input

One year after the vanilla Seq2Seq was first proposed, Bahdanau et al. introduced the attention mechanism to address the information bottleneck problem. The attention mechanism is simple - instead of craming the entire input sequence into a single context vector, we simply create one context vector for each word in the input sequence, and use weights to link each context vector to every cell in the decoder.

The attention mechanism works by allowing the decoder to selectively pay attention to certain inputs, ignoring other inputs that are irrelevant for the current time step. Intutively, this makes sense. When translating the start of a sentence, we should pay attention to words at the start and vice versa when translating words at the end.

Visually, this is what a Seq2Seq model with attention mechanism does when translating a sentence:

The weights of the attention mechanism allows the decoder to selectively use certain parts of the input at each time step. Mathematically, we define the weights of the attention mechanism between \( c_i \) and \( h_j \) as \( \alpha_{ij} \). The context vector \( c_i \) is then computed as a weighted sum of all the attention weights:

$$ c_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \alpha_{ij}h_j $$

where \( n \) refers to the number of words (i.e. time steps) in the input. Bahdanau et al describes this attention mechanism as learning to align and translate.

Attention is all you need

The attention mechanism greatly improved a wide range of NLP tasks, including translation. In fact, the significant improvement of Google translate can be attributed to deep learning and the attention mechanism.

Research efforts quickly poured into the attention mechanism, which led to the next breakthrough in NLP - the transformer model. In my next blog post, we will take a look at the transformer model in greater detail.
